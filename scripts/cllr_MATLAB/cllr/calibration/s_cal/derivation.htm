Derivation of S-Cal

 

Even if an un-calibrated detector gives a score of very large magnitude, no reasonable person would believe that it could give a very high degree of certainty about the speaker hypothesis. Intuitively we know that current technology cannot extract an arbitrarily high degree of certainty from noisy speech. If the detector construction is such that scores of excessively high magnitude may occur, it seems we have to compensate for this in the calibration stage. The following is a possible way to quantify an intuitive limit to the degree of certainty that a detector can give.

 

We are given some input data, x, which consists of two segments of speech. We want to infer from this input to which of the following two primary hypotheses the speech data x can be attributed:

 

·       H1: both segments were produced by the same (one) speaker.

·       H2: these segments were produced by two different speakers.

Then, we are given a detector D, which despite best efforts to train it to recognize H1 and H2 , in fact recognizes the following two (weaker) alternate hypotheses:

 

·       : The quality of the speech input x is such that it sounds like*  the two segments are of the same speaker.

·       : The quality of the speech input x, is such that it sounds like the two segments are of different speakers.
